# Cooling Credit Stakeholder Payment Model

This document explains **who can pay for Cooling Credits, why they would pay, and what kind of outcome they should receive in return**.

Cooling Credits should not rely only on voluntary goodwill. They become durable when the payer has a rational reason to fund measurable cooling outcomes.

---

## 1. Core Principle

The basic financial principle is:

```text
Those who suffer from heat-related losses should be able to fund verified cooling outcomes before the losses occur.
```

Cooling Credit finance should therefore be treated as a form of:

- climate-risk reduction finance;
- heat-damage prevention finance;
- public-health prevention finance;
- disaster-prevention investment;
- infrastructure resilience investment;
- natural cooling-function restoration investment.

---

## 2. Main Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Reason to Pay | Expected Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Municipalities | Heatstroke, disaster recovery, infrastructure damage, public-space decline | Lower heat risk, lower public costs, safer urban spaces |
| National governments | Climate adaptation, disaster prevention, food and water security | Reduced national risk and lower long-term public expenditure |
| Companies | ESG, worker safety, supply-chain stability, local operating risk | Verified cooling contribution and risk reduction |
| Real-estate developers | Heat island mitigation, property value, tenant comfort | Cooler districts and improved asset value |
| Utilities | Peak electricity reduction and grid stability | Lower cooling demand and smoother load curves |
| Insurers | Lower claims from heat, flood, drought, and infrastructure damage | Reduced insured losses |
| Agriculture and food companies | Soil moisture, yield stability, drought risk | More stable production areas |
| Tourism operators | Outdoor comfort, landscape recovery, heat-risk reduction | Maintained or restored tourism value |
| Fisheries and coastal communities | Ocean heat, oxygen depletion, coastal ecosystem risk | Improved coastal resilience and ecosystem conditions |
| Investors | Outcome-linked climate investment | Measurable social, environmental, and financial return |
| Local communities | Public health, comfort, livelihoods, disaster prevention | Community cooling and local benefit sharing |

---

## 3. Payment Logic by Stakeholder

### 3.1 Municipalities

Municipalities already pay for heatstroke response, public cooling shelters, disaster recovery, infrastructure repair, flood control, drought response, and emergency support.

Cooling Credits can convert part of these reactive expenditures into preventive investment.

```text
Pay after damage → Pay before damage to reduce damage
```

### 3.2 Companies

Companies may participate when Cooling Credit projects reduce operational risk, protect workers, stabilize supply chains, improve local reputation, or support verified ESG and sustainability reporting.

However, payment must not become an excuse for continued emissions. Cooling Credits should be reported as cooling contribution, not emissions offset permission.

### 3.3 Insurers

Insurers have a direct interest in reducing claims from heat waves, floods, droughts, crop loss, property damage, and infrastructure failure.

A verified reduction in climate-related damage can justify premium-linked or outcome-linked payments.

### 3.4 Utilities

Utilities may support Cooling Credits when projects reduce peak cooling demand, stabilize the grid, lower emergency reserve needs, or reduce local overload risk.

### 3.5 Investors

Investors may participate through project funds, municipal bonds, outcome-linked finance, blended finance, or infrastructure investment.

Investor return should be linked to verified outcomes, not speculative certificate trading.

---

## 4. Payment Mechanisms

Possible payment mechanisms include:

| Mechanism | Description |
|---|---|
| Direct credit purchase | A buyer pays for verified Cooling Credits |
| Outcome-based contract | Payment is made when measured cooling outcomes are achieved |
| Municipal cooling contract | A municipality funds local cooling performance |
| Insurance-risk reduction payment | Insurers pay when projects reduce verified loss risk |
| Utility demand-reduction payment | Utilities pay for verified reduction in peak cooling demand |
| Public-private partnership | Public and private actors jointly fund cooling infrastructure |
| Cooling bond | Bond proceeds fund cooling projects, with reporting linked to MRV |
| Blended finance | Public money reduces risk for private investment |
| Community benefit fund | Part of revenue returns to local maintenance and benefit sharing |

---

## 5. Buyer Classification

Cooling Credit buyers can be divided into three types.

### 5.1 Damage Avoidance Buyers

These buyers pay because heat and climate damage directly cost them money.

Examples:

- municipalities;
- insurers;
- utilities;
- agriculture and food companies;
- tourism operators;
- real-estate owners.

### 5.2 Responsibility and Disclosure Buyers

These buyers pay to demonstrate verified contribution to climate adaptation, urban cooling, public health, or ecological recovery.

Examples:

- corporations;
- ESG funds;
- universities;
- public institutions;
- large event operators.

### 5.3 Strategic Infrastructure Buyers

These buyers pay because cooling infrastructure protects long-term regional stability.

Examples:

- national governments;
- development banks;
- infrastructure funds;
- climate funds;
- international organizations.

---

## 6. What Buyers Should Receive

A Cooling Credit buyer should receive:

1. a clear project description;
2. a baseline explanation;
3. measured cooling indicators;
4. MRV report;
5. risk and uncertainty disclosure;
6. validity period;
7. local benefit statement;
8. statement that the credit is not an emissions offset unless separately certified under another system.

---

## 7. What Buyers Should Not Receive

Cooling Credits should not grant:

- permission to emit CO2;
- automatic net-zero claims;
- vague ESG claims without data;
- ownership of public natural functions;
- speculative rights disconnected from physical cooling;
- permanent claims for temporary cooling outcomes.

---

## 8. Summary

Cooling Credit finance becomes credible when payment is linked to measurable cooling outcomes and rational loss reduction.

The central question is:

```text
Who benefits from lower heat, lower disaster risk, lower cooling demand, and restored natural cooling functions?
```

Those beneficiaries are the logical payers for Cooling Credits.
