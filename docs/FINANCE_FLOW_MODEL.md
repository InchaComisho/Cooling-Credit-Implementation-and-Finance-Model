## Overview

This document describes the funding circulation model required to make Cooling Credits operational.

Cooling Credits should not depend only on corporate goodwill. They should be designed as a pre-damage investment mechanism in which parties exposed to heat and climate losses pay to reduce future damage.

---

## 1. Basic Structure

```text
Funding providers
↓
Cooling project fund / SPV / municipal project
↓
Cooling project implementation
↓
Outcome measurement through MRV
↓
Cooling Credit issuance
↓
Outcome payments, purchases, subsidies, and investment returns
↓
Operations, reinvestment, investor return, and community return
```

---

## 2. Funding Providers

Potential funding providers include:

- governments;
- municipalities;
- international organizations;
- development banks;
- climate funds;
- insurance companies;
- utilities;
- real-estate and urban-development companies;
- agriculture and food companies;
- tourism operators;
- fisheries and coastal municipalities;
- ESG investors;
- corporate CSR and sustainability budgets.

---

## 3. Why Pay?

The reason to pay is not abstract environmental goodwill. It is concrete loss prevention.

Cooling outcomes may reduce:

- heat-related medical costs;
- disaster-recovery costs;
- cooling electricity demand;
- blackout risk;
- agricultural damage;
- water-shortage risk;
- insurance payouts;
- loss of real-estate and commercial value;
- tourism decline;
- fishery and coastal economic loss.

---

## 4. Revenue Sources

A Cooling Credit project should diversify revenue sources.

Possible sources include:

- outcome payments from municipalities;
- government subsidies;
- risk-reduction payments from insurers;
- peak-demand reduction payments from utilities;
- value-uplift payments from real-estate and development companies;
- benefit-sharing from agriculture, tourism, and fisheries;
- ESG and CSR budgets;
- Cooling Credit sales;
- interest-payment sources for cooling bonds.

---

## 5. Private Investment Model

```text
Investors
↓
Cooling project fund
↓
Multiple cooling projects
↓
Cooling outcomes and economic benefits
↓
Investor return
```

Investor returns should be based on real project outcomes, not certificate speculation.

Possible investment targets include urban cooling infrastructure, soil recovery, forest restoration, water-cycle restoration, tourism-resource recovery, coastal and fishery recovery, and dryland greening.

---

## 6. Blended Public and Private Finance

Cooling Credits can be implemented more easily when public and private funding are combined.

```text
Government subsidies
+ municipal disaster-prevention budgets
+ private investment
+ insurance-risk reduction payments
+ corporate ESG funding
+ Cooling Credit revenue
```

This blended model lowers early-stage risk and supports regional pilots.

---

## 7. Outcome-Based Contracts

Outcome-based contracts pay when agreed indicators improve.

Possible indicators include WBGT reduction, surface-temperature reduction, lower cooling electricity demand, lower heatstroke cases, increased rainwater infiltration, reduced flood damage, stabilized agricultural yields, and reduced insurance payouts.

---

## 8. Community Return

Cooling Credits should not return benefits only to investors.

Community return may include maintenance reinvestment, local employment, recovery of agriculture, tourism and fisheries income, better public spaces, cooling of schools and hospitals, and reduced medical or electricity burdens.

---

## 9. Preventing Speculation

Cooling Credits may eventually connect to finance, but measured outcomes must come first.

Important safeguards include issuance only after measured outcomes, third-party verification, registry management, anti-double-counting, retirement after use, restrictions on exaggerated claims, and prevention of misuse as emissions offsets.

---

## Conclusion

The finance flow model for Cooling Credits is a mechanism for making nature restoration economically beneficial.

It is not a donation model. It is a prepaid risk-reduction investment for reducing heat, disaster, medical, energy, agricultural, water-resource, and insurance losses.
