# Local Versus Global Cooling Credit Effects

Current simulation graphs can look modest because they measure bounded project economics:

1. A single city project does not change regional climate.
2. A single farm project may not repay without public or supply-chain support.
3. A single dryland mist node may not create rainfall.
4. A single coastal project may not reduce cyclone intensity.
5. Thousands of coordinated cooling, water-retention, soil-recovery, forest, dryland, urban, coastal, and ocean projects may change background risk conditions—but this must be tested.

| Scale | What it can show | What it cannot show |
|---|---|---|
| Local pilot | Local temperature, WBGT, runoff, cost, payback | Continental atmospheric feedback |
| Municipal program | Urban heat, public health, flood burden | Global water-cycle stabilization |
| Regional watershed | Flood peaks, soil moisture, agriculture | Global climate moderation |
| Continental dryland network | Heat-gradient moderation, evapotranspiration recovery hypothesis | Guaranteed rainfall |
| Global implementation | Distributed cooling and hydrological stabilization potential | Deterministic weather control |

## Two complementary appraisals

> Local simulations measure direct project economics. Global stabilization scenarios measure systemic avoided risk. Both are necessary.

Local MRV prevents vague planetary claims and supports stop/scale decisions. Regional and global models test nonlinear interactions, displacement, teleconnections, and uncertainty that local cash-flow models cannot contain. Systemic benefit should not be counted as local credit revenue unless a defensible attribution and payment contract exists.

The conceptual risk-pressure simulation is therefore a comparison lens, not evidence that deployment will cause the plotted outcome.
