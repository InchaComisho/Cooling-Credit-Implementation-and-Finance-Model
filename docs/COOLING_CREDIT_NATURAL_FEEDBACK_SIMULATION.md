# Cooling Credit Natural Feedback Simulation

## Purpose

This document defines a conceptual simulation model for comparing **carbon-credit-style accounting systems** with **Cooling Credit systems based on physical cooling and natural feedback restoration**.

The purpose is not to claim that all carbon credits are ineffective. Some projects may reduce emissions or support useful mitigation. However, if global CO2 emissions remain high while credit markets expand, the gap between accounting value and planetary physical outcome must be made visible.

Cooling Credits are designed to measure a different type of outcome:

```text
measured cooling,
existing heat-load reduction,
water retention,
evapotranspiration recovery,
soil and forest regeneration,
ocean-atmosphere circulation support,
and local economic co-benefits.
```

---

## Core Comparison

| System | Main evaluation target | Main weakness | Cooling Credit alternative |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carbon Credit | Emission reduction, removal, or offset accounting | Physical climate outcome may be indirect, delayed, or difficult to verify at planetary scale | Evaluate physical cooling and natural cooling-function recovery directly |
| Sunshade / Reflect credit | Reduced incoming sunlight or increased reflection | Does not necessarily reduce existing heat load or restart water-cycle cooling | Separate as sunshade, shade, reflect, or heat-input suppression |
| Cooling Credit | Measured physical cooling and natural feedback restoration | Requires MRV and local measurement | Directly links climate action to heat, water, soil, vegetation, and local benefits |

---

## Simulation Logic

The model compares five stylized scenarios:

1. **Accounting Offset Scenario**
   - Carbon-credit-style offset revenue grows.
   - Global or regional physical heat indicators do not necessarily improve.

2. **Urban Mist Cooling Scenario**
   - Ultrasonic mist cooling fans and water-cycle urban cooling reduce local heat stress.
   - Results depend on humidity, water source, airflow, and MRV.

3. **Organic Waste to Humus Scenario**
   - Food loss, leaves, organic waste, and biomass residues are dried, crushed, sanitized, and decomposed into humus or compost.
   - Soil water retention, microbial activity, evapotranspiration, crop yield, and waste-incineration avoidance improve.

4. **Forest Regeneration Scenario**
   - Degraded or monoculture forest is gradually converted into mixed forest, native forest, food forest, or managed circular forest.
   - Benefits include water retention, cooling, reduced disaster pressure, forest products, tourism, and local employment.

5. **Integrated Natural Feedback Scenario**
   - Urban cooling, humus regeneration, forest recovery, soil moisture restoration, and water-cycle recovery interact.
   - Once circulation begins, the system can shift from intervention-heavy to maintenance-oriented.

## Core Feedback Loop

```text
Organic matter and microorganisms recover
↓
Soil water retention improves
↓
Vegetation and forest recovery increases
↓
Evaporation and transpiration increase
↓
Latent heat transport increases
↓
Cloud and rainfall recovery pressure improves
↓
Surface heat stress declines
↓
Water returns to soil, rivers, farms, forests, and ecosystems
↓
Natural cooling feedback becomes easier to maintain
```

## Main Indices

| Index | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Physical Cooling Index | Air, surface, water, or WBGT cooling effect |
| Water Retention Index | Improvement in soil, urban, forest, or watershed water retention |
| Evapotranspiration Recovery Index | Recovery of evaporation and plant transpiration |
| Soil Microbial Recovery Index | Recovery of microbial and organic-matter functions |
| Waste-to-Humus Conversion Rate | Share of organic waste converted into humus or compost |
| Urban Heat-Island Reduction Index | Reduction of urban heat accumulation |
| Disaster Pressure Reduction Index | Reduction of heat, runoff, dryness, and local extreme pressure |
| Local Economic Co-benefit Index | Agriculture, tourism, employment, forest products, water savings, and waste savings |
| Cooling Credit Value | Composite value based on physical cooling, duration, MRV, natural recovery, and co-benefits |

---

## Simplified Credit Value Formula

```text
Cooling Credit Value
= measured cooling effect
× affected area
× duration
× MRV confidence
× natural cooling-function recovery factor
× local co-benefit factor
```

Unlike carbon offset accounting, this formula requires a measured physical effect or verified natural cooling-function restoration.

---

## Policy Interpretation

A Cooling Credit system can support:

- municipal cooling subsidies;
- organic waste reduction programs;
- food-loss-to-humus projects;
- farm soil recovery;
- forest regeneration;
- water retention infrastructure;
- tourism-area heat reduction;
- local disaster-risk reduction;
- circular wood use;
- community participation.

The key distinction is that the value is not created by a ledger alone. It is created when the physical environment changes.

---

## Caution

This simulation is conceptual. It is not a weather forecast, hydrological forecast, crop forecast, or disaster prediction model.

Real projects require:

- local meteorological data;
- soil measurements;
- land-surface temperature data;
- water retention data;
- biodiversity and ecological risk review;
- MRV design;
- independent verification;
- long-term maintenance planning.
