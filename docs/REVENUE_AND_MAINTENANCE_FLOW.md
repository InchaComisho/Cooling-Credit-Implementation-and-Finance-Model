# Cooling Credit Revenue and Maintenance Flow

This document defines how revenue from Cooling Credits should be circulated back into operation, maintenance, MRV, community benefit, and reinvestment.

A Cooling Credit system becomes credible only when revenue does not stop at certificate issuance. It must support the physical systems that continue to produce cooling outcomes.

---

## 1. Core Principle

The core principle is:

```text
Cooling revenue must maintain cooling function.
```

If credit revenue is disconnected from maintenance, the system may become a paper market rather than a physical cooling mechanism.

---

## 2. Basic Revenue Flow

A basic revenue flow can be structured as follows:

```text
Buyers / outcome payers
↓
Cooling Credit purchase or outcome-based payment
↓
Project account / SPV / municipal program
↓
Allocation to:
  1. operation and maintenance
  2. MRV and reporting
  3. project debt or investor return
  4. reinvestment and expansion
  5. local community benefit
  6. reserve fund for failure, drought, repair, or ecological response
```

The allocation ratio may vary by project type, but every system should reserve funds for maintenance and monitoring.

---

## 3. Recommended Allocation Categories

| Category | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Operation | Daily operation of cooling infrastructure or restoration activities |
| Maintenance | Repair, replacement, pruning, soil care, water system upkeep, sensor upkeep |
| MRV | Measurement, reporting, verification, data management, third-party review |
| Investor return | Return for risk-bearing capital where applicable |
| Community return | Local benefit sharing, public-space improvement, employment, education |
| Reinvestment | Expansion or improvement of cooling projects |
| Risk reserve | Drought response, equipment failure, ecological correction, emergency repair |
| Governance | Audit, legal compliance, public disclosure, anti-greenwashing review |

---

## 4. Maintenance by Project Type

### 4.1 Urban Cooling

Maintenance may include:

- mist system cleaning;
- rainwater tank inspection;
- pump and pipe repair;
- pavement performance checks;
- tree and green-space care;
- sensor calibration;
- public-space safety checks.

### 4.2 Soil and Agricultural Cooling

Maintenance may include:

- compost and humus supply;
- soil moisture monitoring;
- cover crop management;
- microbial recovery monitoring;
- irrigation efficiency checks;
- farmer support and training.

### 4.3 Forest and Vegetation Cooling

Maintenance may include:

- thinning and mixed-species management;
- invasive species control;
- fire-risk management;
- watershed and soil-moisture monitoring;
- biodiversity monitoring;
- community forest management.

### 4.4 Ocean and Coastal Cooling

Maintenance may include:

- sensor buoys;
- water-quality monitoring;
- dissolved oxygen monitoring;
- ecosystem-response checks;
- device maintenance;
- local fishery and coastal-community monitoring.

---

## 5. MRV Cost Recovery

MRV is not an optional cost. It is the foundation of credit credibility.

MRV costs should be built into the revenue flow from the beginning.

Possible MRV cost items include:

- sensors;
- satellite or remote-sensing analysis;
- data storage;
- field surveys;
- third-party verification;
- uncertainty analysis;
- public reporting;
- audit and governance review.

A project that cannot pay for MRV should remain a pilot or community project rather than a formal credit system.

---

## 6. Maintenance-Linked Validity

The validity of Cooling Credits should be linked to the continued operation of the cooling function.

| Case | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Cooling system operates and MRV continues | Credits remain valid within the verified period |
| Maintenance stops | New credit issuance should pause |
| Sensors fail | Credit issuance should pause until data quality is restored |
| Cooling effect declines | Credit volume should be reduced |
| Ecological risk appears | Emergency review and possible suspension |
| Project expands successfully | Additional verified credits may be issued |

---

## 7. Community Benefit Sharing

Cooling projects often affect public spaces, local ecosystems, and community life.

A portion of revenue should support local benefit sharing, such as:

- public-space improvement;
- local employment;
- school and community education;
- heat-health support;
- farmer or fisher support;
- local maintenance groups;
- disaster-prevention activities;
- transparent local reporting.

Community return helps prevent Cooling Credits from becoming an external extraction model.

---

## 8. Anti-Failure Reserve

Cooling projects face uncertainty: drought, equipment failure, storm damage, maintenance gaps, ecosystem imbalance, and funding delays.

A reserve fund should be created to respond to:

- unexpected repair;
- drought-year water shortages;
- ecological correction;
- sensor replacement;
- emergency maintenance;
- project underperformance;
- community compensation where appropriate.

---

## 9. Revenue Flow Diagram

```text
Cooling Credit buyer / outcome payer
        ↓
Verified cooling outcome payment
        ↓
Project account / SPV / municipal fund
        ↓
+----------------------+----------------------+----------------------+
| Operation            | MRV and reporting    | Maintenance          |
+----------------------+----------------------+----------------------+
| Investor return      | Community return     | Reinvestment         |
+----------------------+----------------------+----------------------+
| Risk reserve         | Governance / audit   | Expansion            |
+----------------------+----------------------+----------------------+
        ↓
Maintained cooling function
        ↓
Next monitoring cycle and future credit issuance
```

---

## 10. Summary

A Cooling Credit system should not end at the sale of a certificate.

It should create a circular financial structure:

```text
Cooling outcome → Verified credit → Revenue → Maintenance → Continued cooling outcome
```

This circularity is essential for preventing paper-only financialization and preserving real-world cooling performance.
