## Overview

Cooling Credits are verified units for reduced heat load, restored natural cooling, and lower disaster risk. A credible system therefore requires project registration, conservative baselines, additionality, MRV, independent verification, issuance, retirement, controls against double counting, and transparent governance.

## 1. System Purpose

- Direct climate finance to measurable cooling outcomes
- Value heat-risk reduction that emissions accounting does not capture
- Connect public funding, private investment, and community adaptation
- Reduce medical, energy, agricultural, insurance, and recovery losses

## 2. Participants

Participants may include project operators, communities, municipalities, government agencies, companies, investors, insurers, utilities, developers, farmers, tourism and fishery businesses, MRV providers, independent verifiers, and registry operators.

## 3. Project Registration

Registration should disclose the project owner, location, intervention, expected outcomes, indicators, baseline, MRV and maintenance plans, financing, risks, safeguards, and evidence of community consultation.

## 4. Additionality

A project is additional when Cooling Credit finance enables it to begin earlier, expand, continue, maintain assets, or perform MRV beyond what would otherwise occur. Legal obligations and business-as-usual activity must not be credited without a justified incremental outcome.

## 5. Baseline

Baselines may use pre-project measurements, comparable untreated sites, multi-year records, weather-adjusted values, official statistics, remote sensing, and historical energy, health, disaster, or insurance data. Assumptions must be public, conservative, and periodically updated.

## 6. Issuance Units

Eligible evidence can include heat-load reduction, WBGT improvement, cooled surface area, lower cooling demand, restored soil moisture, added infiltration, avoided-risk scores, or a composite Cooling Credit Score. Early schemes should avoid forcing unlike outcomes into one exaggerated conversion factor.

## 7. Registry

Each unit needs a unique ID, project and location, monitoring period, indicators, verified values, verifier, owner, transfer history, and status. The registry must be auditable and prevent duplicate issuance or claims.

## 8. Retirement and Use

Used units must be retired permanently. Claims may describe financing for adaptation, cooling, or risk reduction, but must not imply that ongoing emissions have been offset or made harmless.

## 9. Governance

Methodologies, data, conflicts of interest, verification rules, appeals, community safeguards, monitoring, and registry records should be transparent. Authority to approve methods, issue units, verify projects, and hear complaints should be institutionally separated.

## 10. Conclusion

Trust comes from measurable cooling outcomes, not from the certificate itself. Institutional design must remain connected to local climate, water, health, energy, agriculture, and disaster data.
