# Insurance and Cooling Credit Risk-Reduction Model

## Insurance proposition

If heat, flood, drought, and infrastructure risks can be measurably reduced, insurers and reinsurers can treat Cooling Credit projects as risk-reduction investments—not as proof that all claims will fall.

Expected annual loss (EAL) equals the sum, across events, of event probability × loss severity. A project changes EAL only where evidence supports a change in frequency, vulnerability, or severity. Avoided EAL is baseline EAL minus post-project EAL, net of uncertainty and residual risk. A risk premium also includes expenses, capital cost, model uncertainty, and margin, so premium reduction need not equal avoided EAL.

## Risk pathways

| Pathway | Possible measure | Insurance relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Flood peak | Storage, infiltration, runoff coefficient, depth | Property/business interruption EAL |
| Heatstroke | WBGT exposure-hours, incidents, facility cooling | Health/workers’ compensation |
| Property heat | Surface/indoor temperature, equipment stress | Property and equipment breakdown |
| Agriculture | Soil moisture, drought loss, yield variance | Crop/index insurance |

Projects can complement parametric insurance: verified sensors may define heat, rainfall, soil-moisture, or runoff triggers. Basis risk must be tested; project performance and claim triggers should remain distinct.

## Why insurers may pay

An insurer may fund baseline studies, premium-linked rebates, resilience grants, or multi-year outcome payments when the discounted reduction in expected claims and capital volatility exceeds payment, verification, and residual-risk costs. Payment should be conditional on independently verified physical performance, not credit price.

## Data and safeguards

Require geocoded exposure, hazard histories, event loss, vulnerability curves, asset and occupancy data, weather and hydrology, health/privacy controls, maintenance logs, sensor calibration, control sites, and uncertainty bounds. Monitor adverse selection, moral hazard, model drift, correlated catastrophe, maintenance failure, benefit leakage, and double counting between premiums, credits, and public claims.

Start with a sandbox using historical back-testing and a pre-agreed EAL methodology. No model output is an actuarial forecast, pricing instruction, or guarantee; regulated actuaries and local authorities must approve use.
