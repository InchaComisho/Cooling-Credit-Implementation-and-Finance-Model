# Government Decision Brief: Cooling Credits as Prepaid Risk Reduction

## Decision

Authorize a bounded, independently evaluated municipal pilot and a cross-agency baseline. Do not begin with a broad credit mandate.

Governments already pay after heat waves, floods, droughts, medical emergencies, infrastructure damage, and agricultural losses. Cooling Credit implementation shifts part of that burden from post-disaster recovery to pre-damage risk reduction.

## Public value case

- **Fiscal burden:** compare discounted no-action expenditure with capital, operations, MRV, and residual losses.
- **Healthcare:** shaded access routes and cooler public facilities may reduce exposure and heatstroke burden; use health data with privacy controls.
- **Disaster recovery:** infiltration, storage, vegetation, and soil recovery can lower runoff peaks and expected loss, without replacing drainage standards.
- **Public facilities:** weather-normalized meter data can show cooling demand and peak reduction.
- **Water cycle:** retention and soil moisture can reduce runoff and irrigation demand; water quality and scarcity constraints remain binding.
- **Agriculture and food security:** improved moisture and input efficiency may stabilize yields, but crop and climate evidence must be local.
- **Regional resilience:** local construction, maintenance, comfort, tourism, and asset protection can produce co-benefits; avoid double counting.

## Why public funding is justified

Many benefits are public goods, distributed across agencies and realized later than ordinary budgets. Baseline data, open MRV, vulnerable-community access, and early pilots therefore merit public funding. Co-funding should follow the beneficiary principle: utilities, insurers, property owners, and investors pay where contracted savings accrue.

## Safeguards and first action

Require additionality, conservative baselines, control sites, uncertainty disclosure, maintenance reserves, procurement transparency, community consent, no double issuance, registry retirement, and periodic stop/scale gates.

Within 90 days, appoint a lead office and health–environment–water–finance working group; select one priority site and control; approve a 20-year common appraisal template; fund baseline measurement; and issue an open request for pilot design and independent verification. Treat model results as screening evidence, not a budget forecast.
