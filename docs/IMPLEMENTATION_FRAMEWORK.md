## Overview

This document defines the basic implementation framework for applying Cooling Credits to cities, farmland, forests, watersheds, coastal zones, tourism areas, and other climate-vulnerable regions.

Cooling Credits are not issued for bookkeeping claims. They are issued only after measurable cooling outcomes, recovery of natural cooling functions, or reduction of climate-damage risk has been measured, reported, and verified.

---

## 1. Implementation Principles

Cooling Credit implementation should follow these principles:

- place the physical project before the credit;
- issue credits only after measurement and verification;
- adapt methods to local climate, water, land use, and ecological conditions;
- monitor side effects and ecological risks;
- begin with small pilots and expand only after evidence is accumulated;
- align benefits among residents, municipalities, operators, investors, and ecosystems.

---

## 2. Basic Flow

```text
1. Select the target area
2. Identify heat, drought, flood, disaster, and economic-loss risks
3. Design cooling interventions
4. Measure the baseline
5. Implement the project
6. Verify results through MRV
7. Issue Cooling Credits for verified outcomes
8. Circulate funding through outcome payments, purchases, subsidies, and investment returns
9. Maintain, monitor, and reinvest
```

## 3. Early Implementation Domains

### 3.1 Urban Cooling

Suitable early targets include schools, hospitals, elderly-care facilities, station plazas, shopping streets, parks, dense residential areas, and industrial or logistics zones.

Typical interventions include street trees, shade, rainwater use, water-retentive pavement, rooftop and wall greening, recycled water, mist cooling, and organic-matter circulation.

### 3.2 Soil Recovery and Farmland Moisture Retention

Projects may include humus restoration, composting, microbial recovery, increased rainwater infiltration, drought-damage reduction, and yield stabilization.

### 3.3 Forest and Green-Space Restoration

Projects may restore evapotranspiration, water retention, mixed forests, biodiversity, and watershed cooling functions.

### 3.4 Coastal and Ocean Support

Projects may support reduction of marine heat risk, improvement of oxygen conditions, recovery of coastal water quality, and restoration of fisheries and tourism resources.

---

## 4. Implementation Units

Cooling Credits can be implemented at multiple scales:

- facility scale;
- block or district scale;
- watershed scale;
- farmland scale;
- forest-plot scale;
- coastal-water scale;
- tourism-destination scale;
- municipal scale.

In the early stage, small and clearly measurable units are preferable.

---

## 5. Outcome Evaluation

Outcomes should not be evaluated by a single indicator only.

For urban cooling, a useful evaluation set may include:

- air temperature;
- WBGT;
- surface temperature;
- cooling electricity demand;
- heatstroke cases;
- rainwater infiltration;
- green coverage;
- pedestrian comfort;
- public-space usage.

A composite evaluation prevents a project from optimizing one metric while ignoring other risks.

---

## 6. Expansion Strategy

```text
Small pilot
↓
MRV data accumulation
↓
Outcome-based contracts with municipalities, companies, or insurers
↓
Regional project fund
↓
Expansion to multiple regions
↓
Cooling bonds or outcome-linked finance
```

---

## 7. Implementation Cautions

Cooling Credits must not promise exaggerated or universal cooling effects.

Because climate, hydrology, ecosystems, land use, and community structures differ by region, each project should start small, measure carefully, and expand only after the results are confirmed. Ocean, forest, and dryland projects require especially careful ecological monitoring and local consent.
